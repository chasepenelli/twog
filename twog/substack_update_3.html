The Sorafenib Surprise

This one caught us off guard.

Sorafenib is a drug that’s already available through veterinary oncologists. It works by jamming multiple protein targets at once — VEGFR, PDGFR, RAF, c-KIT. Think of it as throwing a wrench into several of the machines that cancer cells rely on to grow and build blood supply.

Last week, sorafenib’s score in our pipeline was 54.2 out of 100. By Friday, it had hit 100.0 — blowing past doxorubicin, which has held the top spot for months with 500 papers and decades of clinical use behind it.

What happened? A wave of new evidence, including a paper specifically about sorafenib combined with low-dose cyclophosphamide in dogs with advanced cancer. The pipeline saw this, weighted it, and kept stacking the score upward.

Here’s the honest part: a score of 100 almost certainly means our scoring system hit its ceiling, not that sorafenib is definitively better than doxorubicin. When a compound with 40 papers outscores one backed by 500, that’s a red flag for the math, not a proclamation about the medicine. We’re already flagging the scoring function for recalibration — adding a literature-volume dampener so that no compound can sprint past drugs with decades of data behind them on a single week of evidence.

The signal underneath is real. Sorafenib covers three of our seven targets. There’s direct clinical data in dogs. It’s available right now. That’s worth watching — even if the score that flagged it was inflated.

Sorafenib’s score jumped from 54.2 to 100.0 in a single week. The scoring function is being recalibrated.

A Drug That’s Already Approved for Dogs

The pipeline flagged something else this week that stopped me: tigilanol tiglate, sold under the name Stelfonta.

This is not a theoretical compound. It’s not a molecule sitting in a simulation. It is already FDA-approved for use in dogs — specifically for mast cell tumors. It works by disrupting the blood vessels that tumors depend on and activating the immune system against the tumor at the same time.

Why does that matter for hemangiosarcoma? HSA is a cancer of blood vessel cells. Anything that targets vascular biology is directly relevant to this disease. The paper the pipeline surfaced describes how tigilanol tiglate kills cancer cells in a way that also triggers an immune response — a one-two punch that hits exactly where HSA lives.

Score right now: 54.2. It’s early. But the fact that it’s already approved, already characterized in dogs, and directly relevant to the vascular biology of hemangiosarcoma makes it worth a real conversation with your oncologist. That’s the kind of thing I wish someone had told us about three months ago.

83,448 Molecules and the One That Matters Most

The pipeline has now designed 83,448 molecules from scratch. These are compounds that don’t exist in any pharmacy, anywhere in the world. Built atom by atom to fit a specific protein target in canine hemangiosarcoma.

This week’s top molecule is GRF-DL-28715, with a composite score of 1.015 — the highest we’ve ever recorded. That composite grades a molecule on everything at once: how well it binds to the target protein, how drug-like it is, how easily a chemist could actually synthesize it, and whether it has properties that would let it reach its target inside a living body. A score above 1.0 is exceptional. This is the first molecule to cross that threshold.

Our validated lead — the one we’ve put through additional confirmation tests — is still GRF-DL-9871. Docking score: -10.49 kcal/mol. Zero Lipinski violations. Zero PAINS alerts. Zero ADMET flags. Highly synthesizable. It targets PIK3CA, the gene mutated in roughly half of all canine hemangiosarcoma tumors.

What the pipeline also showed us this week, clearly and uncomfortably, is a problem we need to fix.

All eight of our top AI-designed molecules target the same protein. PI3K alpha. Every single one. That’s a concentration risk — if the PI3K hypothesis is wrong, or if tumors develop resistance, we have no backup plan in a different target class.

Next week, we’re forcing the designer to diversify. At least 20% of generation cycles will go to VEGFR-2. Another 10% to PDGFRA. Another 10% to AKT1. The PI3K series is mature enough — over 80,000 molecules deep. It’s time to spread the risk. We owe that to every family that might someday depend on what comes out of this pipeline.

The target concentration problem. All top molecules hit the same protein. We’re fixing this next week.

The Vorinostat Question

Our top combination strategy has been alpelisib + vorinostat. Alpelisib is a PI3K inhibitor — it blocks the protein our molecules are designed to target. Vorinostat is an HDAC inhibitor — it works by changing which genes a cancer cell can turn on and off. Together, the idea was to hit the tumor from two completely different angles. Score: 90.67. On paper, it covers ten pathways.

Then the pipeline surfaced this paper: “Manipulating Histone Acetylation Leads to Adverse Effects in Hemangiosarcoma Cells.”

That title alone is enough to pause.

If HDAC inhibition — the exact mechanism vorinostat uses — causes “adverse effects” specifically in hemangiosarcoma cells, we need to understand what that means. Does it mean the expected cell death, which would be what we want? Or does it mean unexpected pro-survival or pro-metastatic effects, which would be dangerous? The word “adverse” in the title implies something the researchers didn’t anticipate.

We don’t have the full answer yet. Full-text retrieval and analysis of this paper is the number one priority for next week. If HDAC inhibition turns out to promote HSA progression rather than fight it, our top three combination strategies all need to be reassessed.

That’s not a failure. That’s exactly what a research pipeline is supposed to do: find the questions before you spend money on the wrong answers.

In the meantime, propranolol + alpelisib looks like the most pragmatic backup. Propranolol is already used in dogs, it’s inexpensive, well-tolerated, and its anti-angiogenic mechanism complements PI3K inhibition cleanly — without the HDAC question mark hanging over it.

Confidence: Where We Actually Stand

6.5 / 10
Overall confidence in therapeutic thesis

What’s working:

PI3K is the most recurrently mutated pathway in canine hemangiosarcoma — about half of all tumors carry PIK3CA mutations. Our lead molecules are validated, drug-like, and synthesizable. The combination strategy is supported by both canine and human angiosarcoma literature. We’ve covered 1,531 papers and designed 83,448 molecules. That’s real coverage.

What’s not:

Every top molecule targets one protein. Docking scores are computational estimates with known error margins of 2–3 kcal/mol. Our scoring system has a demonstrated bug. Our top combination strategy has an unresolved safety question. And none of this has been tested in a lab yet. Simulations are not experiments.

What would change our confidence:

Experimental binding data on GRF-DL-9871. Validated leads against VEGFR-2. Resolution of the vorinostat question. And a scoring system we can fully trust.

We’re being transparent about 6.5 because the families reading this deserve transparency. You’ve had enough people tell you things that turned out to be incomplete. That’s not what this space is for.
