## From "Looks Good on Paper" to "Ready for the Lab"

Think of it like this: the first phase of our pipeline is an architect sketching thousands of key designs for a specific lock. The new phase we just built is a stress-testing facility. We take the best keys and ask:

- Does the key stay in the lock when you shake it? *(molecular dynamics simulation)*
- Does the key accidentally open 30 other doors you don't want opened? *(selectivity profiling)*
- Will the key survive the journey to the door — or dissolve on the way there? *(safety and absorption testing)*
- Has someone already patented this key? *(intellectual property search)*
- Does the key work better with other keys we have? *(combination therapy modeling)*
- Is this key designed for the right species? *(canine-specific protein modeling)*

TWOG now runs all of these checks automatically on every drug candidate before it moves forward. Each piece reads from the same shared database, works in parallel, and contributes to a go/no-go recommendation for each compound.

---

## What We Found

We tested our top 5 candidates. All five passed safety screening. All five show oral feasibility — meaning they could potentially be given as a pill. All five have workable safety profiles.

The molecular dynamics simulations — where we literally simulate the drug molecule interacting with its protein target over time — gave us our first real challenge. The simulations flagged areas that need deeper investigation. This is exactly what a validation step is supposed to do. Better to find questions now, computationally, than after spending thousands on synthesis.

We also generated 57 backup compounds. If any lead fails in the lab, we already have the next candidates scored and ready to go. That's not just efficiency — it's how real drug development programs work. One compound is a bet. A series of compounds is a program.

## The Patent Landscape

Our IP scout searched patent databases for anything that might cover our molecules. One of our candidates — GRF-DL-14543 — has a completely clear patent landscape. Others share structural similarity with existing patents, which doesn't stop research but is important to know early. This is the kind of intelligence that saves months of legal headaches later.

## Why This Matters

Hemangiosarcoma is the most common cancer in many large dog breeds. Median survival after diagnosis is weeks to months. There are no targeted therapies approved for it. The standard of care hasn't changed meaningfully in decades.

We're building something different. An autonomous system that reads the latest research, designs drug candidates, and validates them — continuously. It doesn't sleep. It doesn't forget. It doesn't get discouraged by negative results; it learns from them and designs the next round.

Our top compound, GRF-DL-14542, targets PI3K — a protein that drives cancer cell survival in virtually every hemangiosarcoma tumor. No one has built a PI3K-selective inhibitor specifically designed and validated for dogs before. We're not waiting for human oncology drugs to trickle down to veterinary medicine. We're building from scratch, for the right species, from day one.
